**The AM‑GM Inequality**

The *Arithmetic Mean–Geometric Mean* (AM‑GM) inequality is a fundamental result in elementary algebra and analysis. It relates two different ways of averaging a collection of non‑negative numbers:

- **Arithmetic Mean (AM)** – the ordinary “average” you learn in school.
- **Geometric Mean (GM)** – the “average” you get when you multiply the numbers together and then take a root.

---

## 1. Statement

For any \(n\) non‑negative real numbers \(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n\),

\[
\boxed{\displaystyle 
\frac{a_1+a_2+\dots +a_n}{n}\;\;\ge\;\;
\sqrt[n]{a_1a_2\cdots a_n}}
\]

with equality **iff** all the numbers are equal: \(a_1=a_2=\dots =a_n\).

In words: **the arithmetic mean is always at least as large as the geometric mean**.

---

## 2. Why “non‑negative”?

Both sides involve real numbers. The geometric mean requires taking an \(n\)‑th root, which is only defined (as a real number) for non‑negative products when \(n\) is even, and the product itself must be non‑negative for the inequality to make sense. Hence we assume \(a_i\ge 0\).

---

## 3. Simple cases

### 2 numbers (\(n=2\))

\[
\frac{a+b}{2}\ge\sqrt{ab}
\]

Proof (by squaring both sides, which preserves the inequality because everything is non‑negative):

\[
\left(\frac{a+b}{2}\right)^2\ge ab
\;\Longleftrightarrow\;
a^2+2ab+b^2\ge 4ab
\;\Longleftrightarrow\;
(a-b)^2\ge 0,
\]

which is always true, and equals zero only when \(a=b\).

### 3 numbers (\(n=3\))

\[
\frac{a+b+c}{3}\ge \sqrt[3]{abc}.
\]

A common proof uses the 2‑variable case repeatedly, or the method of *weighted AM‑GM* (see below).

---

## 4. Proof ideas

There are many proofs; here are a few classic ones.

### 4.1. Induction + 2‑variable case  

Assume the inequality holds for \(n\) numbers. For \(n+1\) numbers, group the first \(n\) numbers into their arithmetic mean \(A\) and apply the 2‑variable case to \(A\) and the \((n+1)^{\text{st}}\) number.

### 4.2. Jensen’s inequality (convexity)  

The function \(\ln x\) is concave on \((0,\infty)\). By Jensen,

\[
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln a_i \le \ln\!\Big(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i\Big).
\]

Exponentiating both sides yields the AM‑GM inequality.

### 4.3. Rearrangement / Chebyshev  

Consider the product \(\prod (a_i + t)\) for a positive parameter \(t\) and expand; the coefficient of \(t^{n-1}\) gives the inequality.

### 4.4. Weighted AM‑GM  

If you have non‑negative weights \(w_1,\dots,w_n\) with \(\sum w_i = 1\),

\[
\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i a_i \;\ge\; \prod_{i=1}^{n} a_i^{\,w_i}.
\]

Setting all weights equal to \(1/n\) recovers the standard AM‑GM. Weighted versions are handy for problems where numbers appear with different “importance”.

---

## 5. Geometric intuition

Imagine a rectangle with side lengths \(a\) and \(b\). Its area is \(ab\). The geometric mean \(\sqrt{ab}\) is the side length of a **square** having the same area. The arithmetic mean \(\frac{a+b}{2}\) is the side length of a **square** whose perimeter equals the perimeter of the original rectangle. Since a square encloses the largest area for a given perimeter, the square built from the arithmetic mean must be at least as large as the square built from the geometric mean—hence \(\frac{a+b}{2}\ge\sqrt{ab}\). The same idea extends to higher dimensions.

---

## 6. Applications

| Area | How AM‑GM helps |
|------|-----------------|
| **Inequalities in contests** | Provides quick bounds; e.g., to maximize/minimize expressions under sum or product constraints. |
| **Optimization** | When you need to maximize a product given a fixed sum (or vice‑versa), the optimum occurs when all variables are equal (by equality case). |
| **Number theory** | Bounding factorials, binomial coefficients, or estimating least common multiples. |
| **Probability & Statistics** | Relating arithmetic and geometric means of non‑negative random variables; used in log‑normal distributions. |
| **Algebraic geometry** | In the proof of the *Muirhead* inequality and Schur‑convexity results. |
| **Physics & Engineering** | In thermodynamics (e.g., relating average temperature to geometric mean of temperature ratios). |

**Example (Optimization):**  
Find the maximum product of three positive numbers whose sum is \(12\).

Let the numbers be \(x, y, z\) with \(x+y+z = 12\). By AM‑GM,

\[
\frac{x+y+z}{3} \ge \sqrt[3]{xyz} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
4 \ge \sqrt[3]{xyz} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
xyz \le 4^3 = 64.
\]

Equality occurs when \(x=y=z=4\). Hence the maximal product is **64**, achieved at \((4,4,4)\).

---

## 7. Quick checklist for using AM‑GM

1. **All terms must be non‑negative.** If you have negatives, consider absolute values or a different inequality.
2. **Identify the number of terms** \(n\). Write the AM as \(\frac{1}{n}\sum a_i\) and the GM as \(\sqrt[n]{\prod a_i}\).
3. **Look for symmetry.** If a problem asks for an extremum under a fixed sum or product, equality case (all equal) is often the answer.
4. **Weighted version** can handle situations where some terms count more than others.
5. **Combine with other inequalities** (Cauchy‑Schwarz, Hölder, Jensen) when a single AM‑GM step isn’t enough.

- **AM‑GM Inequality:** \(\displaystyle \frac{a_1+\dots+a_n}{n} \ge \sqrt[n]{a_1\cdots a_n}\) for \(a_i\ge0\).  
- Equality ⇔ all \(a_i\) are equal.  
- Proven by squaring (n=2), induction, convexity of \(\ln\), or weighted versions.  
- Extremely useful for bounding expressions and solving optimization problems.
